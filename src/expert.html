Question 1: Is it too early to establish a regulatory framework for Internet/OTT services, since internet penetration is still evolving, access speeds are generally low and there is limited coverage of high-speed broadband in the country? Or, should some beginning be made now with a regulatory framework that could be adapted to changes in the future? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 1: The answer to the above question is in the negative for the parent and the nested questions. No regulatory framework is required and no such “beginning’ should be made.

Framing of the question.

At the very outset we wish to record our strong objection to the phrase, “OTT” which is a standard telecom terminology limiting the vast range of Internet platforms and services to the mere way in which it is served over a network. Computer technologists, on the contrary make well accepted distinction between the networks layer (TSP’s), and the applications layer (OTT). Hence, to reduce internet platforms and services to a phrase such as OTT is incorrect. Instead of OTT, this response will use the phrase, “internet platforms and services” which is more appropriate.

This question presumes that, first regulation is absent and second in this absence regulation is necessary. These presumptions are incorrect and based on a wrong reading of facts and applicable laws. The regulatory framework and responses to it are contained in the substantive comments contained below.

Substantive Reply

Internet platforms and services exist in a regulatory regime providing users services at a negligible price or one that is subsidised by contextual advertising based on user data. Any additional regulatory, licensing burden is likely to do one thing only, increase the cost of access and degrade the quality of such interactions. This will inevitably lead to higher prices and more invasive advertising online which is contrary to user interest.

It is surprising that the consultation paper presumes that there are no legal compliances applicable to internet platforms. Internet platforms in this respect are akin to MVAS (Mobile Value Added Services) services where several TSP’s made arguments in the TRAI Consultation on MVAS arguing that no regulation was necessary. They stated that first that MVAS services operate on telecom licensees hence a compliance in terms of any security interests is ensured through licenses itself, and second MVAS services are regulated by general laws. This logically applies to Internet platforms and services equally.

It may be considered as to the jurisdictional basis of the TRAI to consider regulating Internet Platforms and Services as opposed to issuing Network Neutrality regulations on TSPs. There may not be jurisdictional basis under the TRAI Act or even under the Telegraph Act to regulate Internet Platforms and Services. However, Network Neutrality regulations can be easily made, since TSPs are directly governed by the TRAI.

Any licensing or registration, even if soft touch such as the registration in the OSP category will destroy innovation, increase time for roll outs and also increase costs for bootstrapped entrepreneurs. The net effect will be disastrous for the internet economy.

Question 2: Should the Internet/OTT players offering communication services (voice, messaging and video call services through applications (resident either in the country or outside) be brought under the licensing regime? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 2: The answer to the above question is in the negative for the parent and the nested questions. No licensing of any form or kind is necessary for any form of internet platforms and services offering communication services. Such a suggestion is regressive and smacks of a partisan tilt towards TSPs.

Framing of the question.

The framing of the question is incorrect and it again makes presumptions which are incorrectly stated in the consultation paper. It firstly presumes that a neat line exists to differentiate communication services and platforms on the internet from the rest. This is incorrect, many internet services to drive consumer engagement offer communication services incidentally.

It is noticeable, that this question goes beyond mere “registration” to a much a more onerous phrase, i.e. “licensing”. This is incredibly dangerous and a regressive proposal which is likely to go against the interests of users and the adoption of newer and more efficient technologies.

Substantive reply

Low costs of entry are essential for innovation in the communication services market. Internet content and applications market can be understood as a “hit-driven industry,” where hit products like Skype, Whatsapp and others create massive spillover value for users. But extreme uncertainty plagues the creation of distinctly new products, and for every successful product there have been numerous failures. Given that the returns to content production are skewed and the expected value of a new online venture is low, sufficiently low costs of entry have been and continue to be crucial. These costs will rise if any mandatory licensing is enforced even on pure communication services offered through the internet.

Communication services which do operate on the internet layer are served through telecom licensees. Here, there is a well-articulated need for a license for the telecoms, which function as a “pipe”. This requires the utilisation of spectrum that is allocated to a TSP. This can be used for data, voice and sms. The network is agnostic to the transmission however it does require, physical infrastructure, quality of service, payments etc. Hence, the licenses which are played on telecom service providers have a demonstrable need and social necessity. These needs are largely absent from internet platforms and services. Moreover since they are served over existing licensed TSP networks there is no need for an additional license.

Communication services are used incidentally by e-commerce retailers, online matrimony websites etc. which often provide needed anonymity provide such messaging services. It is not understood how such services and their product innovation will be affected.

Corresponding Data Points

There was a license for POP3 email services in India (1990-1993) which were subsequently abolished. With the proliferation of web based email services there were no licenses or additional registrations for web based email providers in India. This not only incentivised greater adoption of lower costs, but also increased consumer choice.

Question 3: Is the growth of Internet/OTT impacting the traditional revenue stream of Telecom operators/Telecom operators? If so, is the increase in data revenues of the Telecom Operators sufficient to compensate for this impact? Please comment with reasons.

Answer 3: The answer to the above question is in the negative for the parent and the nested questions. Even on the data points in the consultation paper which indicate a loss in revenue streams in voice and sms the increase in revenue in data leads to a net profit for TSPs. Present trends and expert reports indicate that data usage will drive growth and revenue for TSPs.

Framing of the question

The question presumes that revenue loss is occurring to a TSPs, “traditional revenue streams”. The traditional revenue streams under a USAL license include data services which are showing a tremendous growth in revenue due to increased adoption of internet platforms and services. Hence, the framing of the question itself is incorrect as it fails to consider revenue from data services a traditional revenue stream.

Substantive Response

The question presumes that Internet platforms and services (even those which offer communication services) act as substitutes for voice and messaging facilities offered by TSPs. This is incorrect. Users utilise each specific platform distinctly where for instance, a VOIP call does not offer the same clarity as a call on a voice platform or an instant messaging application such as WhatsApp is rather used for chatting than a SMS feature which is usually used for notification. Usage over each technology offers distinct benefits and costs which are considered by users. A diversity of sources is healthy and allows a greater degree of consumer choice.

A direct correlation between increased adoption in data over voice and messaging has not been adequately demonstrated. A loss in revenue for messaging and voice may also be attributable to other factors rather than pure substitution or the purported cannibalisation of a TSP’s revenues. Other factors which deserve recognition include, i) TRAI’s Unsolicited Communications Regulations which now prevent SMS’s spam and hence impact revenue; ii) Poor quality of service which cause frequent call drops making calls shorter. These points are documented in data points.

The available data and public statements by various telecom companies itself demonstrates that no case exists of revenue cannibalisation by internet based voice and messaging services. On the contrary data usage are driving demand and greater adoption for telecom networks. Here, it is important that TRAI should not through regulation prevent the benefits of technology from flowing to users in India.

Question 4: Should the Internet/OTT players pay for use of the Telecom Operators network over and above data charges paid by consumers? If yes, what pricing options can be adopted? Could such options include prices based on bandwidth consumption? Can prices be used as a means of product/service differentiation? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 4: The answer to the above question is in the negative for the parent and the nested questions. Any regulatory intervention which permits additional charges on customers on the type of services accessed beyond the access charges at present will not only be against consumer interest by increasing costs but also impair innovation and adoption in the types of services used.

Framing of the Question

This question presumes cannibalisation of revenue as well as a risk to the financial viability of telecom operators by existing revenue models to levy additional costs for access for the type of service. This presumption is incorrect as per the data and various reports cited, including but not limited to financial reports of telecom companies itself in the answer to Question No. 3.

The question lacks specificity when it states that. “Prices based on bandwidth consumption”. At present prices by TSPs for mobile bandwidth as well as fixed line internet access are on the basis of the volume of data usage by users. Additionally TSPs have adopted a practice of Fair Usage Policies (FUP), which even limit the amount of data or regulate speeds in data packs which are marketed to permit, “unlimited” internet access in a given billing cycle.

Substantive Reply

A specific access fee to access a specific service or even a type of service will result in a higher and a non-transparent cost to users. Most internet platforms and services are content rich and cannot be divided into a neat silos of the type of service they provide as per bandwidth consumption. For instance many websites embed videos and even embed links and functionality from online media hosting services. Hence any additional cost beyond mere volume will not have any transparency and will be impossible for users to contemplate and have adequate notice prior to accessing the page itself. Even after such access ceases, many elements of the content may not be visible causing charges which will be impossible if not tough to monitor. This will not only increase costs without any justification but also violate the TRAI guidelines on transparent billing. The Quality of Service (Code of Practice for Metering and Billing Accuracy) Regulations, 2006 expressly cite transparency and notice in the tariff plan which will not be possible to achieve in case of discriminatory billing of traffic.

Economic analysis demonstrates that access fees hinders innovation in the application layer. Any charges which are levied will need to be on a rational basis of differentiation which as stated above is not possible. Even when the basis itself is determined, it will inevitably through regulation favour certain firms, or types of services. Hence, this will hinder innovation and especially disincentivise users from accessing certain platforms and services.

The need for any such additional levy is not well articulated. Data points on this including statements by telecom companies, earnings reports and certification agency statements all indicate that the revenues and profits from data usage are growing. Even the long term debt forecast for telecom companies is projected to be stable. Hence at present any such additional pricing beyond pure connectivity will amount to double dipping at a cost to the user.

Such differential pricing has the ability to fragment the internet. At present most websites to a large degree interlink and cross post content which provides a seamless experience while surfing the internet. This allows greater efficiencies in the sharing of knowledge across multiple internet services and platforms. A differential pricing will prevent any such seamless experience. This proposal also has the tendency to promote larger internet platforms and services in order to promote their services to implement zero rating services to the disadvantage of competing firms with lower capital resources and which may even offer better products.

Question 5: Do you agree that imbalances exist in the regulatory environment in the operation of Internet/OTT players? If so, what should be the framework to address these issues? How can the prevailing laws and regulations be applied to Internet/OTT players (who operate in the virtual world) and compliance enforced? What could be the impact on the economy? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 5: The answer to the above question is in the negative for the parent and the nested questions. No regulatory imbalances exist in the regulatory environment in the operation of internet platforms and services. Prevalent laws and legal regulations apply equally to Internet platforms and services and even enforcement is implemented through them. Any imbalances which do exist is due to the nature of the services itself.

Framing of the Question

The question follows the previous approach of posing a query which frames any response in opposition to seem defensive. This is pertinent to mention given that this and previous questions arise from either an ignorance or misstatement of fact and law.

Prevalent laws and regulations equally apply to Internet platforms and services apply equally to Internet platforms and services as is explained in the response below. Moreover, the economic impact of any further regulation without a well-articulated interest, or one which furthers ambiguity will be disastrous for the Indian technology industry.

Substantive Comments

The question inasmuch it states that, “regulatory imbalances” exist ignores that substantial differences exist between internet applications and services and telecom or ISP licensees. Rather than, “imbalances”, these are distinctions. Here the motivation to impose additional regulation on internet platforms and services is not understood and is neither well-articulated. The same “regulatory imbalances” were cited in the MVAS regulation in which arguments on two specific points were made to prevent mandatory licensing. The first that such services use the pipes of TSPs which are pre-existing licensees and second general laws govern such services.

Prevailing laws and regulations are equally applicable on internet services and platforms.

Most internet specific laws have jurisdictional clauses which make substantive laws applicable to where the content is accessed or the crime is caused. Such extra-territorial provisions which deem jurisdiction in such cases of illegality are contained within the Information Technology Act, 2000 and the Indian Penal Code, 1860.

It is important to mention the Supreme Court of India’s recent pronouncement in Shreya Singhal v. Union of India where it has had occasion to comment on the liability of intermediaries. Most internet platforms and services fall within this category as they do not create content, but provide platforms. Hence, end users most of which are residents in India will be liable. This is not only as per the dicta of the court but the conscious policy decision of the government in enacting Section 79 of the Information Technology Act, which makes such platforms, even if they provide communication platforms immune from liability for the acts of users.

Specific provisions exist for blocking of platforms from access in India as well as issuing letters rugatory for compelling the attendance of an accused. These process provide an adequate balance to international entities which provide.

Any additional concerns on privacy and security are already under consideration of several government committees and have already been commented on including but not limited to the National Cyber Security Policy, 2013. These measures may properly be considered by an interministerial group composing of representatives from the Ministry of Home Affairs, rather in isolation by TRAI.

Data Points

Ratio of the Hon’ble Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal v. Union of India:

At Para 117, “Section 79(3)(b) has to be read down to mean that the intermediary upon receiving actual knowledge that a court order has been passed asking it to expeditiously remove or disable access to certain material must then fail to expeditiously remove or disable access to that material. This is for the reason that otherwise it would be very difficult for intermediaries like Google, Facebook etc. to act when millions of requests are made and the intermediary is then to judge as to which of such requests are legitimate and which are not. We have been informed that in other countries worldwide this view has gained acceptance, Argentina being in the forefront. Also, the Court order and/or the notification by the appropriate Government or its agency must strictly conform to the subject matters laid down in Article 19(2). Unlawful acts beyond what is laid down in Article 19(2) obviously cannot form any part of Section 79. With these two caveats, we refrain from striking down Section 79(3).”

At para 118, “However, Rule 3(4) needs to be read down in the same manner as Section 79(3)(b). The knowledge spoken of in the said sub-rule must only be through the medium of a court order. Subject to this, the Information Technology (Intermediaries Guidelines) Rules, 2011 are valid.”

Question 6: How should the security concerns be addressed with regard to Internet/OTT players providing communication services? What security conditions such as maintaining data records, logs etc. need to be mandated for such Internet/OTT players? And, how can compliance with these conditions be ensured if the applications of such Internet/OTT players reside outside the country? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 6: The answer to the above question is in the negative for the parent and the nested questions. At present enough regulations exist to mandate security conditions and interception of information. Further any proposals for additional regulations is required to consider the privacy interests of users. In cases of internet services and platforms located outside India, in cases of non-compliance, as an extreme measure legal provisions permit the blocking of the domain itself.

Framing of the Question

The question presumes that Internet and OTT Players which provide communication services are not obligated by law to comply with information requests. This incorrect on facts as well as law.

Any proposal to enforce security conditions requires a comprehensive privacy law which as per media reports is being drafted by an interministerial group piloted by the DoPT. Security concerns cannot be addressed in a vacuum without addressing the privacy concerns of users.

Substantive Comments

Much of the communication services which operate are on existing TSP networks who monitor them as per information requests under Rule 419B of the Telegraph Rules as well as under the Information Technology (Procedure and Safeguard for Interception, Monitoring and Decryption of Information) Rules, 2009.

It is relevant to consider that independent obligations on Internet / OTT players exist both for data retention. There exist comprehensive regulation which is presently being relied by State authorities which includes courts, police departments and security agencies. These include the following:

The retention of sensitive personal information as defined under Section 43A of the Information Technology Act, is provided as per Rule 5(4) of the Information Technology (Reasonable Security Practices and Procedures and Sensitive Personal Data or Information), 2011 which states that any, body corporate shall not retain data for longer than necessary unless mandated by a provision of law.

This retention period is then defined for a period of 90 days for the preservation of information and associated records for the purposes of any investigation as per Rule 3(4) of the Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules, 2011.

Rule 3(7) of the Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules, 2011 states that, “when required by lawful order, the intermediary shall provide information or any assistance to Government Agencies who are lawfully authorised for investigative, protective, cyber security activity. The information or any such assistance shall be provided for the purpose of verification of identity, or for prevention, detection, investigation, prosecution, cyber security incidents and punishment of offences under any law for thaw time being in force, on a request in writing stating clearly the purpose of seeking such information or any such assistance.”

Independent provisions exist under other laws which are utilised by law enforcement, security agencies and courts to compel the production of such information including Section 91 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973.

It is submitted that any further regulation to mandate the provision of user information will not only impact internet platforms or services, but will be a breach of privacy of users. This breach has with adequate safeguards and a comprehensive legislation which addresses them.

In cases of non-compliance, the government as well as courts can block platforms and websites as has been done in the past. One of the grounds for which many websites have been blocked include national security and public order. This is as per the provisions which exist under the Information Technology (Procedure and Safeguards for Blocking for Access of Information by Public) Rules, 2009. As an extreme measure, even if websites are located abroad, these legal provisions may be utilised to block access to the platform or service in India itself by issuing an order to TSPs and ISPs.

Question 7: How should the Internet/OTT players offering app services ensure security, safety and privacy of the consumer? How should they ensure protection of consumer interest? Please comment with justifications

Question 9: What are your views on net-neutrality in the Indian context? How should the various principles discussed in para 5.47 be dealt with? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 9: India as a developing nation needs to ensure that network neutrality is mandated by regulation to ensure future growth. Its democratic roots, require a plural and diverse internet.

Framing of the Question

It is regretful that the consultation paper which leads to the framing of the present question follows an approach which gives an appearance of bias in favour of TSPs. Paragraph 5.47 commences from what seems is an elaborate quote of an article in the Economist containing arguments against network neutrality. This purported quote is placed in a box, and gives an appearance that this the Economist’s editorial position. However this is factually incorrect as the Economist article of the same day and on the same topic has been paraphrased and TSP sympathetic arguments have been inserted unilaterally. The article rather than making a broader point against network neutrality in principle states it’s a work in progress which is completely omitted from the consultation paper. [Link]

It would not be out of place to state that this approach gives an appearance of bias to the entire consultation process in favour of TSPs and against users of the Internet. It is hoped that such factual inaccuracies are limited to this and do not extend beyond it.

Substantive Reply

An agreed definition of Network Neutrality is contained by the Global Net Neutrality Coalition in which more than 35 organisations from 17 countries are members as, “Net neutrality requires that the internet be maintained as an open platform, on which network providers treat all content, applications and services equally, without discrimination.” [Link] This would include the following principles which are then substantiated in the data points which are provided:

No blocking by TSPs and ISPs on specific forms of internet traffic, services and applications.

No slowing or “throttling” internet speeds by TSPs and ISPs on specific forms of internet traffic, services and applications.

No preferential treatment of services and platforms by TSPs and ISPs.

These principles, notably the principle of preferential treatment has been under consideration of several foreign jurisdictions. Notable amongst them are Chile and Brazil which have taken a position through regulation to prevent any such preferential treatment, or, “fast lanes” for specific forms of internet traffic. Amongst developed countries, other examples include the recently adopted Open Internet Order by the FCC in the United States and Norway.

Data Points

A model network neutrality framework may include the following conditions made by the Dynamic Coalition on Network Neutrality (which has the support of the Council of Europe) [Link]. The specific model network neutrality model suggested by them includes the following points:

Model Framework on Network Neutrality (initiated by the Council of Europe and developed by the Dynamic Coalition on Network Neutrality)

1) Network neutrality is the principle according to which Internet traffic shall be treated equally, without discrimination, restriction or interference regardless of its sender, recipient, type or content, so that Internet users’ freedom of choice is not restricted by favouring or disfavouring the transmission of Internet traffic associated with particular content, services, applications, or devices.

2) In accordance with the network neutrality principle, Internet service providers shall refrain from discriminating, restricting, or otherwise interfering with the transmission of Internet traffic, unless such interference is strictly necessary and proportionate to:

a) give effect to a legislative provision or court order;

b) preserve the integrity and security of the network, services and the Internet users' terminal equipment;

c) prevent the transmission of unsolicited communications for direct marketing purposes to Internet users who have given their prior consent to such restrictive measures;

d) comply with an explicit request from the subscriber, provided that this request is given freely and is not incentivised by the Internet service provider or its commercial partner;

e) mitigate the effects of temporary and exceptional network congestion, primarily by means of application-agnostic measures or, when these measures do not prove efficient, by means of application-specific measures.

3) The network neutrality principle shall apply to all Internet access services and Internet transit services offered by ISPs, regardless of the underlying technology used to transmit signals.

4) The network neutrality principle need not apply to specialised services. Internet service providers should be allowed to offer specialised services in addition to Internet access service, provided that such offerings are not to the detriment of Internet access services, or their performance, affordability, or quality. Offerings to deliver specialised services should be provided on a non-discriminatory basis and their adoption by Internet users should be voluntary.

5) Subscribers of Internet access service have the right to receive and use a public and globally unique Internet address.

6) Any techniques to inspect or analyse Internet traffic shall be in accordance with privacy and data protection legislation. By default, such techniques should only examine header information. The use of any technique which inspects or analyses the content of communications should be reviewed by the relevant national data protection authority to assess compliance with the applicable privacy and data protection obligations.

7) Internet service providers shall provide intelligible and transparent information with regard to their traffic management practices and usage polices, notably with regard to the coexistence of Internet access service and specialised services. When network capacity is shared between Internet access services and specialised services, the criteria whereby network capacity is shared, shall be clearly stated.

8) The competent national regulatory authority shall:

a) be mandated to regularly monitor and report on Internet traffic management practices and usage polices, in order to ensure network neutrality, evaluate the potential impact of the aforementioned practices and policies on fundamental rights, ensure the provision of a sufficient quality of service and the allocation of a satisfactory level of network capacity to the Internet. Reporting should be done in an open and transparent fashion and reports shall be made freely available to the public;

b) put in place appropriate, clear, open and efficient procedures aimed at addressing network neutrality complaints. To this end, all Internet users shall be entitled to make use of such complaint procedures in front of the relevant authority;

c) respond to the complaints within a reasonable time and be able to use necessary measures in order to sanction thebreach of the network neutrality principle.

This authority must have the necessary resources to undertake the aforementioned duties in a timelyand effective manner.

9) Definitions

a) The “Internet” is the publicly accessible electronic communications network of networks that use the Internet Protocol for communication with endpoints reachable, directly or through network address translation, via a globally unique Internet address.

b) The expression “Internet service provider” refers to any legal person that offers Internet access service to the public or Internet transit service to another ISP.

c) The expression “Internet access service” refers to a publicly available electronic communications service that provides connectivity to the Internet, and thereby provides the ability to the subscriber or Internet user to receive and impart data from and to the Internet, irrespective of the underlying technology used to transmit signals.

d) The expression “Internet transit service” refers to the electronic communications service that provides connectivity between Internet service providers.

e) The expression “Internet traffic” refers to any flow of data packets transmitted through the Internet, regardless of the application or device that generated it.

f) The expression “specialised services” refers to electronic communications services that are provided and operated within closed electronic communications networks using the Internet Protocol, but not being part of the Internet. The expression “closed electronic communications networks” refers to networks that rely on strict admission control.

g) The expression “application-agnostic” refers to Internet traffic management practices, measures and techniques that do not depend on the characteristics of specific applications, content, services, devices and uses.

h) The expression “subscriber” refers to the natural or legal person who has entered into an agreement with an Internet service provider to receive Internet access service.

j) The expression “Internet user” refers to the natural or legal person who is using Internet access service, and in that capacity has the freedom to impart and receive information, and to use or offer applications and services through devices of their choice. The Internet user may be the subscriber, or any person to whom the subscriber has granted the right to use the Internet access service s/he receives. Any legal person offering content and/or applications on the Internet is also an Internet user.

Brazil’s Marco Civil Law provides a model which may be adopted. Especially as a developing country Brazil is facing similar arguments on access and neutrality and it has taken a decision with a strongly framed network neutrality law (link).

Question 10: What forms of discrimination or traffic management practices are reasonable and consistent with a pragmatic approach? What should or can be permitted? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 10: Discrimination should only be permitted for traffic management and ensuring the integrity of the network. Even otherwise any determination beyond it should on a case to case basis determination for compliance and ensuring it is not to the detriment of Internet access services, or their performance, affordability, or quality. In any such determination complete transparency should be maintained.

Framing of the Question

The question proceeds on a basis that discrimination or traffic management should be permitted as a course. Rather it should recognise that any such discrimination should be an exception rather than the rule. The rule as per any network neutrality regulation should be to prevent it.

Any such discrimination including pure traffic management requires a transparent process in which only under limited parameters it is permitted. This will require regular audits and publication of reports by TSPs under the supervision of the TRAI.

Substantive Reply

Faced with a growing call on discriminatory access to the internet, In May 2012, the Body of European Regulators for Electronic Communications (BEREC) published the findings of a joint investigation with the European Commission regarding traffic management. It revealed an increased trend of operators restricting access to services and sites. The most frequently reported restrictions are the blocking and/or throttling of peer-to-peer (P2P) traffic, on both fixed and mobile networks, and the blocking of internet telephony (Voice over IP), mostly on mobile networks [Link]. Hence, there exists every possibility of abuse by TSPs if a general rule is made in favour of permitting traffic management or any discrimination with respect to data on the Internet. Hence, the definition of, “traffic management” should to be specific and limited.

An accountable process is required to be implement any such specific exemptions and carve outs from the general rule which prohibits discrimination. Such a process requires regulatory oversight by TRAI, where all such discrimination is monitored, audited and civil penalties are imposed for any violations. Further, the TRAI should also be open to complaints for Internet users and investigate them in case of any discrepancies in data.

Any further discrimination beyond, “traffic management”, in the form of any continuous practice should only be permitted if it is not to the detriment of Internet access services, or their performance, affordability, or quality. Prior to the roll out of any such discriminatory service, prior sanction and approval of the TRAI or any competent body is essential through a public hearing, conducted transparently on the basis of which a regulatory order is passed either permitting or disapproving such discrimination.

Question 11: Should the Telecom Operators be mandated to publish various traffic management techniques used for different OTT applications? Is this a sufficient condition to ensure transparency and a fair regulatory regime?

Answer 11: Yes, under any network neutrality regulation TSPs should be mandated to not only publish the various traffic management techniques. Here mere transparency though desirable is insufficient. They should also be required to be under a process of audit and submission of data to the TRAI which should be made publicly accessible. Further processes for complaint by end-users for network neutrality violations should also be contemplated.

Framing of the Question

This question is based on the premise that merely transparency in traffic management techniques will translate into a fair regulatory regime and that such discrimination should therefore be permissible. In fact, the Consultation Paper suggests that differential treatment of traffic is more likely to be reasonable if it is clearly disclosed to customers, because it places the decision-making largely in the hands of end-users rather than providers. Such transparency should be coupled with a process for scrutiny for compliance which ensures accountability.

Such traffic management policies should be in line with any network neutrality regulations which substantively permit them. No derogations to this should be permitted and TSPs should not be permitted to self-regulate or form their own traffic management policies substantively.

Substantive Comments

Mere transparency as proposed in this question is desirable though insufficient. Recent studies debunk this argument and find that transparency is far from sufficient to ensure a fair regime. In the UK, where traffic management techniques used for different OTT applications were made public, consumer studies in 2014 found that most consumers did not understand traffic management or use it as a basis for switching operators. Those who did do so comprised a group perceived to be small or insignificant enough that most network operators did not seek to factor them into their product decisions, despite some consumers’ complaints about traffic management.

In India where awareness and activism on issues of net neutrality is considerably less, it is unlikely to play the critical role that the Consultation Paper suggests. Hence, beyond mere transparency there is also the necessity for greater scrutiny and accountability in the entire process. Moreover, penalties and fines should be contemplated on TSPs in cases of violations.

Data Points

The inadequacy of mere transparency is highlighted by the recent throttling of video streaming website OGLE by Airtel which has been publicly documented [Link]. Mere transparency in such instances may not be sufficient. Accountable processes should exist and penalties should be imposed to prevent future instances of violations.

The recent FCC Open Internet Order states that, “36. The Commission may enforce the open Internet rules through investigation and the processing of complaints (both formal and informal). In addition, the Commission may provide guidance through the use of enforcement advisories and advisory opinions, and it will appoint an ombudsperson.”

Question 12: How should the conducive and balanced environment be created such that Telecom Operators are able to invest in network infrastructure and CAPs are able to innovate and grow? Who should bear the network upgradation costs? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 12: This question is answered at extent in the responses contained for Question 1-5. Question 13: Should Telecom Operators be allowed to implement non-price based discrimination of services? If so, under what circumstances are such practices acceptable? What restrictions, if any, need to be placed so that such measures are not abused? What measures should be adopted to ensure transparency to consumers? Please comment with justifications.

Answer 13: No, as a rule Telecom Operators should be allowed to implement non-price based discrimination of services. Content based discrimination is detrimental to the character of the internet and will also then permit deep packet inspection of traffic as a practice leading to concerns on the privacy of users. Also any permitted derogations need to be clear, specific and lead to accountable process for ensuring compliance.

Substantive Comments

Non-price based discrimination would ordinarily mean the degradation in type of services if not a specific service itself. Hence it may be in the form of throttling specific types of traffic (P2P, VoIP) rather than specific applications which may be permissible. Such derogation should be permitted purely as network management measure where the degradation of service is agnostic to a specific application, but may extend to types of applications. Even such a degradation should be with adequate notice to users with the limited objective of ensuring the stability of the network. Beyond transparency as stated below a process for scrutiny, audit, and compliance and in case of breaches, a penalty should be contemplated.

Differentiating between providers of types of content or specific applications (Paid prioritization such as the “fast lane”) should be prevented by regulation and discrimination should be permitted only as congestion management/ Technical network protection. Hence the implementation of business models such as data caps or zero rating i.e. the “cheaper” lane are models which should be prohibited by regulation. Any such traffic discrimination in favour of a specific online platform or service through a private arrangement with a TSP will impact innovation and smaller firms negatively.

Data Points

David Kaye, The United Nations Special Rapporteur on freedom of opinion and expression, has stated that, “It is especially important that the new rules prevent ISPs [internet service providers] from discriminating against some types of content in favour of others, either by slowing down delivery speeds or by creating a fast lane to ensure quicker delivery for only some content providers that have paid extra fees.” [Link]
